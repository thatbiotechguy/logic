The Pedagogical Crisis: Administrative Fatigue and Epistemic Injustice Within domains like computer science and software engineering, assessing complex artefacts requires highly specialized evaluation structures. Determining a student’s ability to implement secure cloud configurations or design normalized relational databases demands precise cognitive and psychomotor mapping. Currently, academic staff lack a centralized logical mechanism to align these specialized continuous assessments with overarching program outcomes.

When institutions attempt to solve this by imposing generic software, it mirrors a structural epistemic injustice. The foundational knowledge of the teaching faculty is bypassed, and compliance tracking and rubric generation are viewed as an imposed institutional penalty rather than an instructional advantage. This creates a “missing chair” governance failure, where the experts meant to drive the curriculum are excluded from the design of the tools they are forced to use.

The CBC-Logic Studio Solution: Emancipatory Co-Design and Offline-First Logic Resolving this bottleneck requires a structural departure from top-down educational technology. CBC-Logic Studio provides a foundational computational modelling mechanism that structurally captures and standardizes the assessment logic of computing educators prior to technological scaling.

The platform architecture comprises two strict phases of operation:

  1. The Offline-First Logic Engine: During adapted agile development sprints, computing lecturers dictate the exact relational mapping between course outcomes, authentic assessment requirements, and grading matrices. This structured curriculum corpus is mapped into a relational competency database that is completely isolated from internet dependency, guaranteeing offline reproducibility.
  2. Controlled Automated Intelligence: Before any artificial intelligence can be deployed to automate the generation of project briefs or grading matrices, the underlying human logic must be explicitly defined. Once the offline logic is validated by the academic staff, the platform integrates an AI automation engine.

AI as an Automator, Not a Dictator Crucially, the artificial intelligence component is strictly constrained to act as an automator rather than a dictator of instructional design. Utilizing the localized curriculum corpus dictated by the faculty, the AI engine accelerates the generation of customized project briefs, tracks student evidence milestones, and drafts complex evaluation rubrics. Such boundary restrictions prevent algorithmic hallucination and ensure the AI acts purely as an accelerator of human-defined pedagogical design. By intentionally restricting the AI layer, the platform preserves the professional accountability of the academic staff while establishing rigorous computational benchmarks to test the AI for technical accuracy and algorithmic bias.

STAKEHOLDER JOURNEYS & SECTOR-SPECIFIC USE CASES

How CBC-Logic Studio Operates in Practice A curriculum intelligence platform cannot exist purely as an administrative abstraction. It must seamlessly integrate into the daily instructional realities of its users. CBC-Logic Studio is structured as a multi-modal ecosystem designed to serve specific stakeholders across Makerere University’s College of Computing and Information Sciences (COCIS) and beyond. Below is a breakdown of how our primary actors interact with, benefit from, and sustain the platform.

The Stakeholder Journeys

  • The Academic Staff Journey (Lecturers and Instructors): Instead of struggling to manually track individual learner competencies across large student cohorts, a computing lecturer utilizes the offline-first logic engine to structure their course. During the preparation phase, they define the exact relational mapping between course outcomes and authentic assessment requirements. Once this localized pedagogical logic is validated, the lecturer deploys the AI automation engine to accelerate the generation of customized project briefs and draft complex evaluation matrices. The system acts as a computation engine, eliminating administrative fatigue and allowing the lecturer to focus entirely on instructional delivery and student mentorship.
  • The Direct User Journey (Computing Students): A student navigating a competency-based software engineering or computer science curriculum interacts with the platform to gain absolute transparency regarding performance expectations. Because complex cognitive tasks are mapped directly to observable assessment evidence, the student understands exactly what professional skills they must demonstrate. Upon course completion, the resulting digital ecosystem guarantees that the student leaves the university with a verified, trackable portfolio of computing competence, directly addressing national industry demands.
  • The Curriculum Administrator Journey (Quality Assurance & NCDC): Curriculum administrators and quality assurance personnel utilize the platform to ensure institutional compliance without imposing top-down technological penalties on the faculty. By tracking the structured relational dataset aligned with the latest global computing competency frameworks, administrators can systematically audit curriculum delivery. The platform naturally transforms academic staff into internal institutional champions, achieving immediate pedagogical buy-in long before formal administrative reviews.

Sector-Specific Use Cases

    • Automated Continuous Assessment Design: Systematically extracting static computing syllabi and converting theoretical knowledge statements into structured, observable professional task mappings.
    • Offline Rubric Generation: Enabling academic staff to build rigorous, reproducible computing assessments completely isolated from internet dependency, ensuring functionality regardless of cloud infrastructure availability.
    • Evidence-Based Portfolio Tracking: Structurally capturing and standardizing the assessment logic of computing educators to dynamically track student evidence milestones against established computing competencies.

Built from Makerere University’s computing classrooms, engineered for offline reproducibility, and designed to eliminate administrative fatigue in competency-based education.
